# NIH Research Supplements to Promote Diversity (PA-23-189)

This is arguably the **most accessible federal research funding for an undergraduate** in cancer research — because it does not require you to win a national competition. Instead, an existing NIH grant gets supplemental money to support *you* as a mentored trainee from an underrepresented background.

## How it actually works (read this first)

You, the trainee, **do not submit the application.** The **Principal Investigator of an active NIH research grant** (your faculty mentor) requests an administrative supplement to add you to their funded project ([PA-23-189](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/pa-files/pa-23-189.html)). This is why building a relationship with a funded mentor matters more than any single deadline.

## Who is eligible (candidate)

Candidates from groups underrepresented in biomedical/behavioral research — including by race/ethnicity, disability, or disadvantaged background — across **all stages**:

- **High school and undergraduate** students (summer and/or academic year).
- **Post-baccalaureate** candidates and master's-degree holders.
- **Predoctoral** students.
- **Postdoctoral** and investigator-level candidates.

Eligibility is established by a signed statement from an institutional official, including citizenship documentation ([PA-23-189](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/pa-files/pa-23-189.html)).

## The parent-grant constraint

The mentor's grant must have enough time left:

- **~1 year** remaining for post-baccalaureate candidates or master's holders.
- **~2 years** remaining for predoctoral candidates.
- **~18 months** for postdoctoral/investigator-level candidates.

(Source: [PA-23-189](https://grants.nih.gov/grants/guide/pa-files/pa-23-189.html).)

## What it pays

The supplement covers the candidate's stipend or salary plus reasonable research and training costs, scaled to the candidate's level. Amounts are not a fixed national figure — they are budgeted into the parent grant.

## Deadlines

**Rolling** — submitted by the PI, reviewed administratively by the funding Institute. Each NIH Institute (e.g., NCI) may have its own internal guidance and timing windows, so **verify at the source / with the relevant Institute's training office.**

## FAQ

**I'm an undergrad — how do I get one?** Find a mentor with an active NIH grant (at HCI, this is common), express your interest, and ask whether they would request a diversity supplement to support you. You provide materials; they submit.

**Is it competitive?** It is reviewed administratively, not in a national peer-review pool, which generally makes it more attainable than an individual fellowship — but the parent grant and mentor must qualify.

**Does it count against my NRSA cap?** Diversity supplements are administrative supplements, distinct from NRSA fellowships; confirm specifics with your Institute.
