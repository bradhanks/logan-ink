tags: ["epidemiology", "clinical-methods", "cancer-prevention"]

**Screening** applies a test to an asymptomatic population to detect disease (or risk) earlier than it would otherwise present, with the aim of improving outcomes through early intervention. **Diagnostic testing** is applied to people who already have symptoms or a positive screening result, to establish whether disease is truly present. The same technology can serve both roles depending on context — a mammogram is screening for a symptom-free woman but diagnostic when evaluating a lump.

The distinction matters in cancer prevention because the two settings demand different test properties and have different stakes. Screening tests favor high **sensitivity** to avoid missing cases and must be safe, inexpensive, and acceptable for mass use; diagnostic tests favor high **specificity** and accuracy because they drive treatment decisions. The pre-test probability of disease is low in screening and higher in diagnosis, which strongly affects how a positive result should be interpreted.

A key pitfall is evaluating screening programs without accounting for **lead-time bias** (earlier detection can inflate apparent survival even with no real benefit) and **length bias** (screening preferentially catches slow-growing, better-prognosis cases). A screen that "finds cancer earlier" is not automatically beneficial; that requires evidence it reduces mortality or morbidity.

For example, a positive stool-based colorectal screen does not diagnose cancer — it flags the person for a diagnostic colonoscopy that confirms or excludes it.
