**Community-based participatory research (CBPR)** is a collaborative orientation to research rather than a single study design. It treats the community affected by a health problem as a co-equal partner — involved in defining the questions, designing the study, collecting and interpreting data, and disseminating and applying findings. Its guiding principles include shared power, mutual learning, building on community strengths, and ensuring the community benefits from the work.

CBPR matters in cancer prevention and population science because it improves the relevance, cultural appropriateness, and uptake of interventions, and helps rebuild trust in communities that research has historically exploited or ignored. By grounding studies in lived experience, it can surface **social determinants** and barriers that outside investigators would miss, advancing **health equity**.

A common pitfall is "tokenism" — labeling a project CBPR while community partners hold no real decision-making power, or engaging them only at the end. Authentic CBPR requires sharing control (and often funding), which takes time and can complicate rigid grant timelines. It is an ethic of partnership, not a checkbox.

For example, a hypothetical CBPR project on colorectal screening might have a community advisory board of local residents help choose outreach messages, set recruitment strategies, and review results before publication.
