# "The Environment and Disease: Association or Causation?"

**Author:** Austin Bradford Hill
**Year:** 1965
**Where:** *Proceedings of the Royal Society of Medicine*, 58, 295–300

## What it is
The presidential address in which Hill laid out the nine considerations (strength,
consistency, specificity, temporality, biological gradient, plausibility, coherence,
experiment, analogy) now known as the **Bradford Hill criteria** for moving from
statistical association toward a judgment of causation.

## Why it matters / what Logan took from it
This is the field's touchstone for the difference between "associated with" and "causes."
Even as an early-career reader, the key takeaway is humility: these are viewpoints to weigh,
not a checklist that proves causation — a frame that underlies every later causal-inference
method.
