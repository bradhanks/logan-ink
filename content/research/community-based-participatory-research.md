# Community-Based Participatory Research in Cancer-Prevention Outreach

## What this area is

Community-based participatory research (CBPR) is an approach in which researchers, organizations, and community members **collaborate on all aspects of a project** — from defining the question to designing the intervention to interpreting results. Rather than studying a community from the outside, CBPR treats community members as partners with their own expertise.

CBPR has been identified as a core strategy for reducing health disparities and was the central research approach used across the National Cancer Institute's Community Network Program Centers, which were charged with reducing cancer disparities in racial/ethnic-minority and medically underserved communities. Its principles include recognizing the community as a unit of identity, building on community strengths, facilitating co-learning, working iteratively, and balancing knowledge-generation with real intervention.

## Why it matters for cancer prevention

Outreach that ignores a community's language, trust, and lived context tends to fail. CBPR is why interventions like Dr. Kepka's Spanish-language *fotonovela* and *radionovela* materials were built **with** the communities they serve. For HPV vaccination and rural screening, this partnership model is often the difference between a program that's adopted and one that's ignored.

## Methods used in this area

- **Genuine community partnership** across the research lifecycle (shared decision-making, co-learning).
- **Qualitative methods** — interviews, focus groups, and community advisory boards to surface needs and barriers.
- **Culturally and linguistically tailored health communication** co-designed with community partners.
