# Rural Cancer-Prevention Access & Screening Disparities

## What this area is

Cancer mortality is higher in rural than in urban parts of the United States — especially for cancers where prevention, early detection, and treatment are effective. Rural residents are less likely to be up to date on recommended screening. Analyses of Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) data found the odds of up-to-date breast-cancer screening were roughly 14–27% lower for rural than urban populations across 2018–2022, with rural gaps also seen for colorectal and cervical screening in earlier years.[confirm: exact figures as cited]

The drivers are structural: rural areas combine lower socioeconomic status with limited physical access to oncology and preventive services. Lower rates of insurance, longer travel distances, and workforce shortages compound the problem.

## Why it matters for cancer prevention

Screening and early detection only prevent deaths if people can actually get screened. Where access is thin, cancers are caught later and outcomes are worse. Understanding rural disparities is essential context for the Mountain West, a largely rural region — and it connects directly to HPV-vaccination work, since the same communities that under-vaccinate often under-screen.

## Methods used in this area

- **Descriptive and analytic epidemiology** on national surveillance datasets (e.g., **BRFSS**) to quantify rural-urban screening gaps and trends.
- **Logistic regression** to adjust for socioeconomic, insurance, and demographic factors.
- **Health-services research** examining access barriers (distance, workforce, insurance) and interventions that reduce them.
