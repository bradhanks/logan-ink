# HPV Vaccination Equity in Rural Utah & the Mountain West

## What this area is

The human papillomavirus (HPV) vaccine is one of the few vaccines that prevents cancer — it protects against the HPV types responsible for nearly all cervical cancer and a large share of several other cancers. Despite that, adolescent HPV vaccination rates lag in many rural and underserved parts of the United States, including the Mountain West.

This is the core domain of the **Mountain West HPV Vaccination Coalition**, founded and directed by Dr. Deanna Kepka (College of Nursing, University of Utah; Huntsman Cancer Institute). Launched in 2014, the coalition convenes a large, multi-state membership — primary-care providers, public-health staff, advocates, and parents — that meets regularly to lower barriers to vaccination across the region.[confirm: current exact membership count, stated by the project as ~500] Earlier in her training, Dr. Kepka developed rural-focused, Spanish-language community interventions, including a *fotonovela* educational pamphlet and a *radionovela*, to reach Hispanic/Latino families.

## Why it matters for cancer prevention

HPV vaccination is primary prevention: it stops infections that would otherwise progress to cancer years or decades later. Closing the rural-urban and language gaps in vaccine uptake is one of the highest-leverage ways to prevent future cervical and other HPV-associated cancers — especially in regions where downstream screening and treatment access is itself limited.

## Methods used in this area

- **Descriptive epidemiology and survey analysis** — using sources like national immunization survey data to track coverage and identify gaps by geography, age, and demographics.
- **Logistic regression** and related modeling to study which factors (provider recommendation, access, language, attitudes) predict whether an adolescent is vaccinated.
- **Health-communication design** — culturally and linguistically tailored materials (e.g., fotonovela/radionovela formats).
- **Community-based participatory research (CBPR)** — partnering with the communities being served rather than studying them from the outside.
